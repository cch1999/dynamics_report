Lay Summary

In haematopoiesis, stem cells in the bone marrow divide and specialise into the blood cells that function in oxygen transportation, immunity and clotting. Dysregulation of haematopoiesis can lead to cancers like leukaemias and lymphomas, which are treated with radiotherapy and/or chemotherapy. This destroys both cancerous and healthy cells; however, marrow transplants can regenerate the marrow to restore blood cell production.

Advances in microscopy allow the tracking of individual or large numbers of transplanted cells in mouse models of marrow regeneration. Techniques from statistical mechanics, usually applied to the motion of particles, can then be applied to cells. This allows us to see whether motion at one time is similar to motion at later times (autocorrelation) and whether cells move randomly or in a more directed way. Additionally, examining cell density variation within the marrow provides quantitative insights into cell colony growth and structure during regeneration.

This study investigated these questions in multipotent progenitor (MPP) cells, which are more specialised than stem cells but still able to turn into multiple blood cell types. Velocity and acceleration did not autocorrelate over short (minute) or long (hour) timescales and their movement was non-random, which may be related to their search for appropriate marrow locations ('niches') before proliferating. However, the MPPs studied are those that remained within the microscope’s limited field of view, and so may not be a representative sample. During proliferation, overall density increases but with local variations. Further experimental and analytical work is required to strengthen these conclusions, but this work illustrates the utility of methods borrowed from physics in studying fundamental cellular behaviour that may in future help in the assessment and improvement of marrow transplantation.

Methods: Data were analysed from mouse models of irradiation and marrow transplantation. MPPs (n = 10) were tracked by laser scanning confocal microscopy to calculate velocity, acceleration, their

Results: MPPs’ velocity and acceleration showed no short- (three-minute) or long-ranged (8-hour) autocorrelation. They initially exhibited superdiffusive motion that appeared to transition to subdiffusion, although likely artefactually. Global density increased with time, and relative density decayed with distance but with no clear temporal ordering.

Conclusion: MPPs showed no short- or long-ranged velocity or acceleration autocorrelation and diffused anomalously, violating the PRW model. These findings might be explained by their search for niches, although further experimental and analytical work is required. Density increased globally across time with variable local decay. This study illustrates the utility of biophysical approaches in examining cellular behaviour that may in future aid the development of marrow transplantation.
